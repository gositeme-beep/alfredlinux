🔍 What's Actually Happening

When you connect through a home or public Wi-Fi provider, other parties with access to that network can see, intercept, and interfere with your traffic. Automated requests are being sent through your connection — either by someone on your network, by software installed on a shared device, or by your internet provider's own infrastructure.


This is unauthorized surveillance and unlawful interception of communications. You are not the one doing this — but your connection is being used as a vehicle for it, and our rate limiter triggered because it saw too many requests from your network address.

🔐 How to Fix This Right Now

1

Turn off Wi-Fi on your phone and use your mobile carrier's data (4G / 5G). This routes your traffic through a completely different network that the offending party does not control.

2

Use Your Phone as a Hotspot

Need a laptop? Enable Personal Hotspot (iPhone) or Mobile Hotspot (Android) in settings and connect your computer to it instead of the compromised Wi-Fi.

3

Try Logging In Again

Once on cellular data, return to gositeme.com and try again. It should work immediately.

ⓘ Why does cellular data work?
Your cellular carrier assigns you a different IP address completely separate from your home / public internet provider. The interfering party has no control over your cell network. By switching, you bypass their infrastructure entirely.

💬 Switch to Encrypted Messaging (Signal + Veil) ▼
  1. For post-quantum protection: use Veil — Kyber-768 encryption that's built to resist even future quantum computers
  2. For broader compatibility: download Signal from signal.org
  3. Enable Registration Lock in Signal (Settings > Account) to prevent number hijacking
  4. Enable Disappearing Messages on sensitive conversations
  5. Alfred AI connects to both Signal and Veil — your AI assistant works across both
🔎 Check Your Device for Spyware ▼
  1. Download Malwarebytes (free version)
  2. Run a full system scan (not quick scan)
  3. Remove anything it flags
  4. Check browser extensions — remove anything you don't recognize
  5. Check installed apps — look for anything you didn't install
  6. On phone: Settings > Apps — review the full list

Signs of spyware: Battery draining fast, device running hot when idle, data usage spikes, unfamiliar apps, camera/mic activating unexpectedly.

🔑 Lock Down Your Accounts (2FA) ▼
  1. Install an authenticator app: Google Authenticator, Authy, or Bitwarden Authenticator
  2. Go to each important account's security settings
  3. Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) — select "Authenticator App"
  4. Scan the QR code with your authenticator app
  5. Save backup codes somewhere safe (printed paper, not on compromised device)

Avoid SMS-based 2FA — SIM swapping attacks can intercept texts. App-based 2FA is much safer.

📡 Secure Your Home Wi-Fi Router ▼
  1. Access router admin (usually 192.168.1.1 or 192.168.0.1)
  2. Change the admin password from the default
  3. Set Wi-Fi encryption to WPA3 (or WPA2 minimum)
  4. Change your Wi-Fi password to something long and unique
  5. Disable WPS (Wi-Fi Protected Setup) — known vulnerability
  6. Disable remote management
  7. Check for firmware updates
  8. Review connected devices — remove anything unrecognized

If your ISP controls your router and you can't change settings, consider buying your own. A compromised router exposes everything on your network.

🛡 Protect Yourself Going Forward

⚖ Know Your Legal Rights

Unauthorized interception of electronic communications is a federal crime under:

  • Electronic Communications Privacy Act (18 U.S.C. § 2511) — prohibits unauthorized interception
  • Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (18 U.S.C. § 1030) — prohibits unauthorized access to systems
  • Stored Communications Act (18 U.S.C. § 2701) — prohibits unauthorized access to stored communications

If you're experiencing persistent interference, you have the right to:

  • File a report with the FBI's IC3
  • File a complaint with the FCC
  • Report to the FTC
  • Contact your state's Attorney General office
  • Consult an attorney specializing in digital privacy and cybercrime
