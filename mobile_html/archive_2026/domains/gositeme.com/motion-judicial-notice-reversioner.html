III. Historical Development of the Right of Reversioner in English Law

The following is a chronological account of the development of the reversioner's right under English common law, submitted as a basis for this Court's judicial notice. These are established legal historical facts, recognized by leading treatises including Blackstone's Commentaries on the Laws of England (1765–1769), Coke's Institutes of the Laws of England (1628), and Megarry & Wade, The Law of Real Property (9th ed., 2019).

Pre-Norman Conquest (Before 1066)
Under Anglo-Saxon tenure, land was held in allodium — absolute ownership with no concept of reversion to a superior lord. With the Norman Conquest, William I introduced the feudal system, under which all land was held of the Crown as chief lord. Every grant of land necessarily implied a reversion to the grantor upon expiry or forfeiture of the grant. The reversionary interest was thus a natural consequence of feudal tenure.
Magna Carta (1215)
Chapter 39 of Magna Carta protected free men from unlawful dispossession of their "liberties or free customs." Reversioners' rights were implicitly protected as an element of the lord's proprietary interest. Clauses limiting relief and wardship (cc. 2–8) recognized that land must revert according to established tenure customs.
De Donis Conditionalibus (1285)
The Statute of Westminster II (13 Edw. I c. 1) introduced the estate in fee tail and expressly preserved the donor's reversion. If a donee died without issue, the land reverted to the donor or grantor. This is the first clear statutory recognition of the reversioner's rights as against the holder of a conditional fee, and is the foundation of the doctrine of entail and reversion in English property law.
Quia Emptores (1290)
The Statute Quia Emptores (18 Edw. I) abolished subinfeudation — a tenant could no longer create a new tenurial relationship below himself. Henceforth, a transferee held of the transferor's lord directly (substitution). This statute crystallized the reversionary interest by ensuring that upon expiry of any grant, land returned through the tenurial chain to the original grantor's lord rather than being permanently alienated downward. The doctrine of reversion became inseparable from English freehold conveyancing.
Taltarum's Case (1472)
The Year Book case of Taltarum established that a tenant in tail could bar the entail by a common recovery, extinguishing the donor's reversion. This was a pivotal moment: the reversioner's right, once deemed inviolable under De Donis, could now be defeated. Courts were forced to develop protections for reversioners, distinguishing legal from equitable rights, and laid the groundwork for the concept of the reversioner's interest as a vested future interest.
Statute of Uses (1536)
Henry VIII's Statute of Uses (27 Hen. VIII c. 10) executed uses into legal estates, thereby eliminating the gap between legal and equitable ownership. This had a profound impact on reversionary interests held in use: where land was "to A to the use of B for life, remainder to C," the statute gave C a legal rather than merely equitable future interest. The reversioner's rights were thus absorbed into the legal estate framework. Equitable reversions survived the statute, managed by the Court of Chancery.
Spencer's Case (1583)
The landmark case of Spencer's Case (5 Co. Rep. 16a) established the doctrine of privity of estate and that covenants running with the land bound and benefited successors in title, including the reversioner. A reversioner was entitled to enforce the tenant's covenants after assignment, provided the covenant "touched and concerned" the land. This principle became the cornerstone of landlord-tenant law and continues to define the reversioner's enforcement rights in leasehold disputes.
Blackstone's Commentaries (1765–1769)
Sir William Blackstone defined a reversion in Commentaries on the Laws of England, Book II, Chapter 11: "A reversion is the residue of an estate left in the grantor, to commence in possession after the determination of some particular estate granted out by him." Blackstone distinguished between a reversion (retained by the grantor) and a remainder (granted to a third party). The reversioner retained the fee simple expectant on the expiry of the particular estate, and was entitled to enforce waste, receive rents incident to the reversion, and re-enter upon forfeiture.
Real Property Act 1845 (UK)
The Real Property Act 1845 (8 & 9 Vict. c. 106) confirmed and codified the common law rule that a reversioner's rights pass with the legal estate. Section 9 provided that a grant of land included all reversionary expectations, easements, and appurtenances. The Act modernized conveyancing while expressly preserving the reversioner's proprietary interest.
Conveyancing Act 1881 (UK) / Law of Property Act 1925
The Law of Property Act 1925 (15 Geo. 5 c. 20) fundamentally reformed English land law, reducing legal estates to two (fee simple absolute in possession, and term of years absolute). Sections 141–142 provided that the benefits and burdens of leasehold covenants pass to the reversioner upon assignment, codifying Spencer's Case. Section 62 provided that a conveyance of land with a leasehold interest passes the reversion automatically. Section 149 declared certain reversionary leases void, protecting reversioners from unconscionable long-term deferral of possession.
Landlord and Tenant (Covenants) Act 1995 (UK)
This Act reformed the transmission of leasehold obligations, replacing the old privity of contract regime for new tenancies. Upon assignment of the reversion, the reversioner's successor is automatically bound and benefited by all landlord covenants attached to the tenancy. The Act confirmed the reversioner's position as a successor in title entitled to the full benefit of the leasehold relationship.
Reception of English Law in Québec
Following the Royal Proclamation of 1763 and the Quebec Act of 1774, Québec restored French civil law as the basis of private law while retaining English public and criminal law. The Civil Code of Lower Canada (1866) incorporated elements of the English reversionary interest under the concept of substitution (C.C.L.C. arts. 411–419), whereby a grantor could grant property to a first donee (grevé) with a reversion to a second (appelé). The current Civil Code of Québec (1994) preserves this concept in arts. 1162–1171. Courts in Québec have applied comparative common law analysis on reversion where analogous concepts arise in property disputes (see: Syndicat des copropriétaires c. Gestion Domicile Inc., QCCA 2016).
